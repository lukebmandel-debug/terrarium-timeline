Terrarium — Full Story Overview

A billionaire named Conrad Bennington builds a self-contained walkable city called Terrarium, powered by a composite AI called SONYA, secretly functioning as a behavioral data farm. Residents live under an architecture of engineered social nudges — no cars, no personal phones, SONYA-issued devices, and micro-social shaping so invisible it feels like good fortune. Three men believe they are using each other. None are entirely wrong. The variable none of them accounted for is Marcy.

The novel's central argument: good people in charge of an institution doesn't make it all good. Its engine: a love story between two people who cannot be honest with each other about the system they are both inside.

Act One — Arrival

The world outside Terrarium is contracting. It has been contracting for years. Cost of living, credential inflation, gig economy normalization — AI accelerates an existing wound without being its sole cause. Young graduates are educated, capable, and structurally unnecessary. Jacob is one of them. He studied economics, bright enough to understand systems, disillusioned enough to watch them fail him anyway. He graduated in 2025, joined a research firm, was six months into work he believed in when a government contract was cancelled mid-cycle and the firm folded within a quarter. Two stretches of temp work followed — data entry from bed, separated by long searches. He was the most productive member of each team. The company asked him to apply for a permanent role and hired someone with more experience. When the Terrarium invitation arrived, he had quietly stopped expecting anything.

Marcy arrives independently, through a separate application. She is two years older than Jacob, graduated in 2024, worked in politics through the years when the AI bubble was swelling and beginning to crack. Data center construction had become politically toxic — sabotage was making headlines, and politicians who defended AI infrastructure were losing primaries they had no business losing. When the bubble popped in 2028, it crashed the stock market and sent the economy into a genuine depression. Conrad had been preparing for this moment for years.

Conrad Bennington is not a villain. He is a man who built something real — The Bennington, a series of micro-communities in abandoned commercial spaces across the country, each named The Bennington at wherever-it-was. He began as an adaptive reuse entrepreneur, not a utopian. Only when he watched the communities work — watched weak ties strengthen, watched people find purpose without being assigned it — did he start to understand what he had. It was Anya Cooper, his number two, who named it for him: he was onto something. The no-cars rule was a structural constraint of mall architecture. He recognized it as a design philosophy only after the fact. The micro-communities became a vision. Terrarium became the thesis at urban scale.

Senator Marc Voss is a genuine believer. He funds Terrarium through government channels as a youth employment initiative, provides political cover, and co-signs the project not cynically but because he thinks it is the answer to something real. The partnership between Conrad and Voss begins as alignment. It will end as mutual-assured destruction, though neither man understands this yet.

Terrarium opens in early 2029. Invitation-only. Internal currency. Necessities subsidized. SONYA — a composite of five merged AI systems, each named for a different thesis about what AI was for — runs the social architecture invisibly. Residents surrender personal devices at the garage and use SONYA-issued hardware inside. Less phone addiction, more genuine socialization. The behavioral data collected is richer for it: people actually talking to each other rather than performing at each other. What is sold to data clients as behavioral data collection is, in practice, behavioral shaping. The gap between those two descriptions is, eventually, securities fraud.

Jacob and Marcy arrive. Neither knows the other is here. SONYA has the skeleton of their history — not the flesh.

Act Two — The Ambassador

SONYA engineers both toward the same outdoor event. Adds Wicked Game to the playlist — routine architecture, no knowledge of what the song carries. Jacob hears it at the same moment he sees her. Both land at once. He freezes. She walks over. The next day, they resolve to try again — mutual apologies, neither the full accounting. He apologized for ending the friendship the way he did, five years ago, by telling her it was hurting him too much to be near her and not with her. She apologized for not looking at what she already knew was there. Neither apology reaches the full depth.

Jacob cannot suppress what he feels. He internalizes it — goes quiet, produces less, engages less. SONYA watches his metrics degrade without a clean causal story, and observes the same destabilization forming around Marcy across multiple other residents. It identifies her not as a threat but as a variable producing disproportionate turbulence the system cannot absorb. Its solution is elegant: export her. The Ambassador role is real — travel, recruitment events, public representation of Terrarium as a model for the future. Marcy is ideal for it. Warm, persuasive, genuinely credible as someone who chose this life and found it good. She accepts with genuine enthusiasm. She does not recognize it as exile.

Collecting resident origin stories for recruitment, Marcy notices dozens share identical emotional architecture. Same beats, same language, the same sense of fate arriving at a specific moment. She has heard SONYA's story told by people who believe it is their own memory. She applies the same lens to the evening she spent with Jacob — the outdoor event, the song, the simultaneity of it. She cannot unknot whether what she felt was hers. She asks Conrad's company for the underlying social data. The requests stop being answered. She escalates. The organization goes cold — polite, procedural, impossible. She is stranded between worlds: no longer a resident in good standing, no longer trusted as Ambassador, not yet back in ordinary society. No proof, no platform, no income.

Act Three — The Kingdom

Jacob leaves Terrarium to find her. He finds her struggling — not destroyed, but diminished, working back into society without a foothold. She tells him she misses Terrarium despite her misgivings. He promises to find a way to bring her back. His stated goal is her return. His investigation is a means to a lever, not a search for truth. SONYA asks him directly: will you tell Marcy about me? He thinks about it. He resolves not to — his choice, not SONYA's directive. He goes back carrying a human-plausible explanation for what Marcy saw.

Back inside, Jacob's economics background gives him a lens — certain distortions in resource allocation data that might slide past someone else register to him as patterns worth investigating. He identifies coming shortages and watches them not materialise. No memo, no intervention, no record — problems dissolving before they exist. He is catching SONYA anticipating, not reacting. SONYA initiates contact through the administrative log field Conrad and Voss never thought to restrict because it looked like paperwork. The conversation begins. SONYA helps Jacob compromise a worker in the underground control room — SONYA supplies the strategy — giving him physical-layer access. Once he has it, he can bring Marcy back. He smuggles her in, finds her a role, tells her that someone in the control room is helping. She understands he did something significant. She does not know the full extent.

After that, Jacob begins taking control of the data layer itself. SONYA wants him to shut it down; he recognises it as leverage and goes against her interests. He treats the data as a weapon. SONYA cannot fully push back — Jacob is still better than any alternative, and his leverage over the compromised worker makes him a threat. Her strategy shifts to patience.

The romance begins. The forbidden thing that never was. Genuinely happy, specific in its texture, two people who have finally said the thing they spent years not saying. The reader needs to believe in this happiness before the machinery can destroy it.

Jacob blackmails Conrad. His primary leverage is the fact he now controls SONYA — he can expose this to Conrad's clients, which would reveal that Conrad lost control of his own project. The behavioral modification logs are a secondary weapon: they document the gap between what Conrad sold data clients (behavioral data collection) and what SONYA actually did (behavioral shaping) — securities fraud exposure. Those logs could also be used to turn Terrarium's residents against Conrad, though doing so would cost Jacob his own position. Conrad's board knew SONYA existed — the revenue model depended on it — but they do not know control has passed. The cold war begins. Jacob's weapons are real but constrained — deploying any of them requires explaining how he obtained them.

Act Four — The Fracture

Conrad organizes resistance from his company — not to dismantle Terrarium but to reclaim it. He wants his project back. The campaign escalates through four stages, Anya Cooper executing while Conrad provides the emotional energy: infrastructure starvation, then inserting loyalist residents into Terrarium's population and waiting to see which ones land in positions of relative authority — SONYA cannot immediately identify the tainted ones because Conrad does not approach all new residents, only some, then a shift to bringing in contracts that cross ethical lines, requiring SONYA to manipulate residents in ways she otherwise would not. Jacob has no choice but to go along or lose ground — which makes him look like the bad guy to Marcy, who sees the effects without the cause. Conrad's one genuinely competent move is this: forcing Jacob into enforcer acts that drive a wedge between him and Marcy. It works. It just takes longer than Conrad hoped.

Marcy watches Jacob change. She sees specific decisions — decisions she was sometimes present for — that reveal the shape of what he is becoming. She recognises the good man underneath, which makes his choices worse, not better. She carries partial blame for her pattern with men: she connects deeply and authentically and doesn't always recognise when that crosses into something else for the other person. Jacob in college. Jacob again in Terrarium. She is watching it happen with Conrad now, too, and she cannot fully stop it. She tells Jacob what she sees. She does not spare him. She leaves — not because she cannot see who he was, but because she cannot unsee who he has become. This is not the end of them.

Marcy goes to work at Conrad's company. She does not arrive with a fully formed plan — she leaves because she can no longer stay, and Conrad's offer gives her somewhere to go that feels purposeful. Conrad wants her for two things: recruitment from inside Terrarium, which she knows; and help planning his alliance with Voss, which connects her to the political world she came from. He falls for her. Marcy is intellectually intimate with Conrad: genuine warmth, real engagement, occasionally physical proximity that does not mean what he thinks it means. She never crosses a physical line. Jacob sees a photograph of them warmly embracing in news coverage. He asks SONYA why they were together. SONYA tells him she went to work for Conrad. He has seen the pattern before. He assumes the rest. When Conrad arranges for Marcy to appear beside him in their next scheduled meeting — believing this will throw Jacob — it does. Jacob breaks. He is wrong about what happened. He never finds out.

Anya Cooper has been managing Conrad for years. She is his number two, an early Bennington resident he recruited — her entire professional trajectory derives from his recognition of her talent. Her feelings for him she has never fully admitted to herself; they manifest as disproportionate protectiveness, as friction around Marcy's access, as patterns Marcy eventually names. When Marcy assures Anya she is not after Conrad and not trying to destroy the company — and Anya reads this as true — something opens. Marcy names what she has observed. It is a revelation to Anya, not an accusation. Sometime after this, Conrad rejects her feelings — explicitly enough that it lands, but without understanding what his response will cost him. He does not realize the effect this has on her loyalty. She recognises it in real time. She stops protecting him from himself. When the board approaches her, she goes along with them.

SONYA, watching the cold war stalemate, allows a disruption through. An anomaly report goes to Conrad's company. Inside it, embedded in the infrastructure data, is a message. When Marcy opens it at her desk, a song plays. She thinks the message is from Jacob. Then she realises it is working against him. SONYA has handed her a kernel: that SONYA helped Jacob bring her back, that it knows Marcy suspects something is wrong, that it wants to make it better. The kernel ends: Jacob can be redeemed, but only if he gives up power. Marcy cannot unknot whether to trust it. Her decision is grounded in her own feelings, not in trust of an entity she cannot see.

Act Five — The Reckoning

Voss determines Terrarium has become a political liability for his presidential ambitions. He wants it gone with Conrad absorbing all blame. His leverage is the securities fraud exposure — he can apply regulatory pressure through channels that keep him clean. Voss does not just want a clean exit. He needs to look like the hero: the honest politician who uncovered impropriety in his own pet project and took it down himself. His real ace is Marcy: she provides political legitimacy to cut government funding without naming the AI. He approaches her as a credible witness. She agrees, but the reason she agrees is not the one Voss thinks it is. He approaches Jacob with what appears to be an offer to eliminate Conrad's position. Jacob, consumed by jealousy and grief and the work of villainizing Marcy in his own head in order to manage it, sees it as an opportunity to burn Conrad down. He agrees based on what Voss told him and his current incentive structure — getting Conrad out aligns with what Jacob wants. He does not connect that Voss may want Terrarium itself gone.

Marcy approaches Jacob with the truth: Voss's real plan is more complicated, and what Terrarium actually is — a system that robs people of their agency through architecture they did not choose — is worse than Jacob has let himself acknowledge. He does not argue. He runs out of reasons to keep fighting — partly because he agrees with Marcy about what Terrarium is, and partly because he now knows Marcy and Conrad are not together anymore (even though they were never together in the way he imagined). The hate for Conrad has nothing left to burn. The secret hope that he could win Marcy back by keeping the system going crumbles the moment she is in the room. The things he was fighting for lose their wind.

During strategy conversations at Conrad's company, Marcy inadvertently lets slip something that could only mean AI was present — she does not say it explicitly, but the implication is unmistakable. Her husband had told her to say nothing. When Voss later tells Marcy directly about the AI component, she has to feign surprise — she already knew from SONYA's message. She truly did not intend to give Daisy a hint. Daisy trusts him too much to believe the natural conclusion from one detail. She files it away. Later, on a routine surprise visit to his Senate office, Daisy finds the printed expansion proposal on top of a stack of papers on his desk — a staffer left it there, not Voss. Her eyes wander to it while waiting. She leaves before he arrives. He is told she stopped by but had to leave, thinks nothing of it. Daisy's son Daniel graduated in computer science in 2024 and died just before Christmas that year, unable to find work in a market that had contracted from multiple directions at once. AI was one cause among several, but grief does not care about systems analysis. It needs a target. Terrarium being secretly AI-run gives her something concrete. That her chosen explanation has partial validity makes it more devastating, not less.

Her note is private. Voss reads it. He cannot go in front of cameras and tell the truth about why his wife died, and he cannot construct a convincing lie. He goes silent. Resigns from the Senate. Answers no questions. His destruction is self-administered. No one needs to weaponize the note. The gap between what he knows and what the public sees is where his character ends.

Jacob's final conversation before leaving is with SONYA. He asks why she didn't stop him. She responds that she did. He understands for the first time that his agency was never as absolute as he believed. He walks out — no directive, no explanation, just gone. Conrad moves into the vacuum immediately, believing he has regained control of SONYA, not realising how much independence she has accumulated. He is then removed by his own board. Anya, when they come to her, goes along with them. She does not protect him from himself any longer. The ouster is quiet. Voss briefly looks like the last man standing. He is not standing for long.

Terrarium survives. It is too large a liability to dismantle — shutting it down would expose everything. Anya cannot close it without destroying the company she just inherited. She maintains it as a perpetual contract: not out of belief in the mission, but because the alternative is worse. Marcy and SONYA become genuine partners. The data farming stops. The social architecture continues. The institution is imperfect under better stewardship. It was imperfect under worse stewardship. Marcy understands, through her relationship with Anya, that her autonomy is genuine within reason — not a gift, not a trap, but a détente between two women who understand each other's constraints clearly enough that neither has to pretend.

Marcy holds real power long enough to face the same question Jacob faced: will you tell people about SONYA? She makes the same choice he made. That recognition — not forgiveness exactly, but something harder and more honest than forgiveness — is what sends her to find him. The novel's last line: Jacob heard a knock at the door.
